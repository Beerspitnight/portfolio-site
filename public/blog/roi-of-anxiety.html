The ROI of Anxiety: What $52 Billion in EdTech Spending Actually Bought Us

By Brian McManus | First Published: 11.22.25

Introduction: A Personal Reckoning

This article was meant to be a straightforward research piece on the timeline of EdTech integration in US k-12 public schools. But yesterday, the research left the electrons of my screen and landed squarely in my living room, with my freshman son as the subject.

Like many parents, I've been trying to coach him on navigating high school while keeping an eye on the online gradebook. Recently, he fell behind in English and Math. Because I hadn't checked the "email me everything" box during setup, I was blissfully unaware. His mother, however, was getting daily updates. Conflict ensued.

When I pulled him over to check his grades, I asked—calmly—"Why do you have two missing assignments in English?"

His response was immediate: defensive, rapid-fire, and full of justifications. Once he finished his closing argument, I asked, "Were they due on the days you were sick?" He paused, calmed down, and confirmed that was the case.

Twenty minutes later, his mother called. I heard the exact same defense from him. And suddenly, I understood the problem.

Grades are supposed to be simple symbols representing academic achievement. (I will spare you my rant on what an 85% actually means compared to an 84%, or how "extra credit" for volunteering, privileges those with free time and transportation). But if a grade is a symbol of achievement, what does it fail to represent? A desire to learn? Work ethic? The willingness to choose a difficult topic over an easy one?

The missing piece of the puzzle was context.

I emailed his teachers. Both replied with kind words about him (though they noted his tendency to find games on his laptop and a need for better note-taking—hello, summer Study Skills program). Crucially, I asked about the deadlines for the missing work. Both teachers were nonchalant: "He can do it next week," or "He can take the test Monday or Wednesday. It's up to him."

The online gradebook didn't say that. It just screamed "MISSING" and flashed a scary "45%" average.

The data was deceptive.
The marking period is two weeks old.
There are only four assignments.
The missing work is worth 1/3 of his grade.
The teacher isn't worried.

Yet, my son has been conditioned to react defensively to the data because the Gradebook has become the ultimate Authority.

We decided to shift the dynamic. Instead of an inquisition over missing checkboxes, we focused on goal setting: What do you want to achieve? How does being proactive support that? It was a lesson in how to be an adult.

But this domestic episode illustrates exactly what my "beast" of a research project uncovered. The timeline below represents the rise of EdTech—not just as tools for learning, but as mechanisms of surveillance and inequity.

The Investment That Changed Everything

Between 2004 and 2021, American K-12 education underwent a quiet revolution. What began as a promise to "bridge the digital divide" transformed into something far more complex: a surveillance infrastructure that would make Jeremy Bentham's panopticon look quaint by comparison.

The Federal Commitment

The federal government invested $37 - 52 billion in broadband and education technology infrastructure during this period—a massive investment that increased tech-based solutions in schools by orders of magnitude.

This wasn't just about infrastructure. It was about fundamentally changing what "school" meant.

The Catalyst: Common Core Testing Requirements

The inflection point came in 2013-2014, when Common Core State Standards testing requirements forced a technological reckoning. The Partnership for Assessment of Readiness for College and Careers (PARCC) and Smarter Balanced Assessment Consortium mandated computer-based assessments for 50 million students—requiring devices with minimum 9.5-inch screens, keyboards, and secure "lockdown" browser capability.

Districts needed sufficient devices for testing windows, rotating students through computer labs, and ensuring every child could access online assessments. The result was a massive wave of Chromebook and iPad purchases, WiFi upgrades, and network security implementations.

By 2016, the technological foundation for ubiquitous classroom surveillance was in place.

From Report Cards to Real-Time Surveillance

By June 1996, I was pretty sure I'd graduated, but I had to do the math to prove it. I remember sitting in class, scribbling numbers as the teacher dictated them, and feeling pure relief when the final calculation landed safely on a 70.
Back then, parents stayed in the dark until report cards arrived. That silence ended in the early 2000s with the launch of platforms like Pinnacle Gradebook.

At my first teaching job, veteran teachers warned against this new transparency. I dismissed their concerns at the time, believing access to data was harmless. I was wrong.

The Unprecedented Shift
Online gradebooks didn't just digitize grades; they inadvertently altered the parent role. Research indicates parents shifted from being supporters of education to "accountability partners", and tech allowed them to be real-time on-demand observers of their child's educational experience.
"For secondary parents, this access is unprecedented, as secondary school historically has been the time when students are expected to become more independent."
— Blair & Yost (2019)

The Unequal Burden of "Connectivity"

But this transformation wasn't equally shared. The cost of staying connected fell hardest on those who could least afford it.

The Affordability Crisis

If low-income households (averaging $13,219/year in 2020) paid the national average broadband price of $77/month, it would consume nearly 7% of their annual income.

This affordability barrier helps explain why 43% of low-income households lack home broadband entirely (Pew Research, 2021). Those who do subscribe often rely on subsidized plans through programs like FCC Lifeline ($9.25/month discount) or provider-specific low-income options (Comcast Internet Essentials at $9.95/month, AT&T Access at $10/month).

In practice, research shows low-income households spend about 2.4% of income on broadband—still nearly five times more than wealthier households (0.5% of income), according to Federal Reserve Bank of New York research.

This disparity in connectivity burden created what researchers call "opportunity hoarding"—middle-class families could leverage school technology seamlessly for homework portals, parent communication apps, and real-time grade monitoring, while low-income families faced difficult tradeoffs between connectivity and other essentials like food, housing, and healthcare.

The Platforms That Filled the Void

With federal infrastructure investment laying the groundwork, commercial platforms moved quickly to capitalize on the new connectivity landscape:

The Perfect Storm (2014-2016)

By 2016, the "transparency ecosystem" was functionally universal in American K-12 education:

  • Parents could check grades, attendance, and behavior in real-time
  • Teachers could broadcast classroom activity instantly
  • Administrators suddenly had visibility into every classroom, every day, all the time
  • Students operated under constant surveillance, with every assignment, behavior point, and attendance mark instantly visible to parents and administrators

The infrastructure (devices, broadband, WiFi) met the platform moment (ClassDojo, Google Classroom, Remind) met the policy requirement (Common Core testing). Digital transformation became inevitable.

The Irony No One Mentions

Here's the part that should give everyone pause: Between 2012 and 2019, U.S. students' PISA (Programme for International Student Assessment) scores in reading, math, and science remained relatively flat or declined slightly.

By 2022, the U.S. ranked 26th in math (up from 29th in 2018)—but this apparent "improvement" came during a pandemic-disrupted testing cycle when many countries' scores declined more sharply than America's. In fact, U.S. math scores declined 13 points between 2018 and 2022; the ranking improved only because other nations fell further.

So what did $37-52 billion in federal spending actually buy?

Not better test scores.
Not stronger academic outcomes.
Not reduced achievement gaps.
But something else entirely: a comprehensive data collection infrastructure that transformed every classroom into a broadcast studio and every student into a data point.

The Surveillance State We Built by Accident

This is the story of how America built an education surveillance state—not through malicious intent, but through incremental policy decisions, market forces, and the genuine belief that "more data equals better outcomes."

It is the story of how we distilled the complexity of education down to a simple symbol that tracks a student's good and bad days. This grade serves as a constant reminder of their "rack and stack" position in the ecosystem of U.S. public education—a system where anxiety, not learning, is the primary motivator.

And we wonder why students jumped at the chance to use AI to complete their assignments.
